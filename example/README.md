## Welcome to the World of Space!

In this lesson, we aren't talking about astronauts and planets—we are exploring the "space" occupied by shapes, lines, and angles. From the hands of a clock to the tiles on your floor, geometry is everywhere. Let’s master how to measure, draw, and solve it!

## 6.1 Angles: The Art of the Turn

An **angle** is formed when two straight lines meet at a common endpoint called a **vertex**. Think of it as a "corner."

### How to Measure and Form Angles

To be precise, we use a tool called a **protractor**. Whether you are using a plastic one from your pencil case or a digital one on your screen, the steps are the same:

1. **Align the Center:** Place the center hole of the protractor exactly on the vertex.
2. **Line up the Zero:** Ensure the baseline of the protractor sits perfectly on one of the lines.
3. **Read the Scale:** Look where the second line crosses the degree marks.

**Skill Goal:** You should be able to form specific angles, such as:

* **45°** (A sharp, acute angle)
* **90°** (A perfect L-shape or "Right Angle")
* **120°** (A wide, obtuse angle)

> **Pro-Tip:** Always check if your angle looks "sharp" (less than 90°) or "wide" (more than 90°) to make sure you are reading the correct scale on your protractor!

## 6.2 Regular Polygons: Perfect Symmetry

A **regular polygon** is a 2D shape where every side is the same length and every interior angle is exactly the same. They are the "perfectionists" of the shape world.

### Meet the Family (3 to 8 Sides)

* **3 Sides:** Equilateral Triangle
* **4 Sides:** Square
* **5 Sides:** Regular Pentagon
* **6 Sides:** Regular Hexagon
* **7 Sides:** Regular Heptagon
* **8 Sides:** Regular Octagon (Think of a Stop sign!)

### Drawing Tools

To draw these accurately, we use different backgrounds:

* **Square Grids:** Perfect for right angles and squares.
* **Isometric Grids:** These use dots or triangles to help you draw shapes from different perspectives.
* **Computer Software:** Using digital tools allows us to create these shapes with pixel-perfect precision.

## 6.2.2 Measuring Interior Angles

Every regular polygon has a specific "inner" angle. There is a mathematical secret to finding them!

### The Formula

To find the interior angle of any regular polygon, use this formula:


$$\frac{(n - 2) \times 180^\circ}{n}$$


*(Where **n** is the number of sides)*

### The Cheat Sheet

* **Triangle (3 sides):** $60^\circ$
* **Square (4 sides):** $90^\circ$
* **Pentagon (5 sides):** $108^\circ$
* **Hexagon (6 sides):** $120^\circ$
* **Octagon (8 sides):** $135^\circ$

## 6.3 Problem Solving: Space in the Real World

Now, let’s put those skills to work! Solving "Space" problems is like being a detective.

### 1. Angles on a Line and Point

* **Straight Line:** All angles on a straight line add up to **180°**.
* **Around a Point:** A full circle of angles adds up to **360°**.

### 2. Perimeter and Area

* **Perimeter:** The total distance around the outside. Just add up all the side lengths!
* **Area:** The space inside the shape. For regular polygons, we often split them into smaller triangles to calculate the total area.

### 3. Volume (3D Shapes)

When a shape has "depth" (like a box), we find the **Volume**. For Cubes and Cuboids, use this simple formula:


$$\text{Length} \times \text{Width} \times \text{Height}$$

> **Example:** A box that is 5cm long, 2cm wide, and 10cm high has a volume of **100cm³**.
